Lesson 05 · Operations

Renewals, rotation & monitoring

Issuing a cert is half the job. The other half is making sure it renews before it expires, that key rotation doesn't break in-flight connections, and that you find out about problems before the cert expires at 03:00 on a Sunday.

The renewal window

cert-manager watches every cert it issued and renews when the remaining validity drops below a threshold. The default is 30 days — i.e. a 90-day Let's Encrypt cert gets renewed at the 60-day mark.

spec:
  # override the default; renew when 1/3 of the duration is left
  duration: 2160h       # 90 days
  renewBefore: 720h     # 30 days before expiry

Why the default is what it is: with a 90-day LE cert and a 30-day window, you get two full renewals worth of buffer before the cert actually expires. If one renewal fails (LE outage, DNS hiccup, controller restart), the cert still has a month to live and cert-manager can retry. The 60-day boundary isn't arbitrary — it's the floor for "two retries in the worst case".

Don't make renewBefore too aggressive. A renewBefore of 1 hour on a 90-day cert means you have ~zero retries. If the first renewal fails, the cert expires and your service is down. Leave at least 7 days of buffer.

What actually happens during renewal

The renewal is a full new issuance — new private key, new cert, new ACME order, new challenge. The Secret is updated in place. Any pod mounting that Secret via projected volume sees the new cert within the kubelet's sync interval (default 60s after the Secret update).

Key rotation is on by default: every renewal generates a new private key. That's safer (forward secrecy) but it means any in-flight TLS connection using the old key gets cut when the Secret updates. The cost is small for HTTP/2 (which can handle brief reconnects) and zero for short-lived calls.

Long-lived connections across renewals. A gRPC stream or a long-poll HTTP request that's mid-flight during a renewal will fail with a TLS error. For most services this is invisible (the client reconnects). For streaming workloads, schedule renewals during low traffic, or pin connections to a specific cert version via a gateway.networking.k8s.io backendTLSPolicy.

The four states a Certificate can be in

StateConditionWhat to do
Ready=Truecert is valid, not yet near expirynothing
Ready=True (renewing)cert is valid, renewal in progressnothing — this is normal
Ready=Falselast renewal failed, cert may be near expirydescribe & fix
Ready=False + expiredcert has passed its notAftereverything is broken — investigate now

cert-manager does not retry forever on a failed renewal. After a few attempts (default 3, configurable via the --max-retries flag on the controller) it gives up and leaves the cert in its last-known-good state. If the cert is then near expiry, you're in trouble.

Monitoring: the metrics that actually matter

cert-manager exposes Prometheus metrics on :9402/metrics on the controller pod. The five to alert on:

1cert expiry < 7 days
2renewal failures
3controller down
4challenge errors
5rate-limit hits

Translated into PromQL:

# any cert expiring in the next 7 days
certmanager_certificate_expiration_timestamp_seconds
  - time() < 7 * 86400

# controller isn't running
absent(up{job="cert-manager"})

# failed renewals in the last hour (LE rate-limited, DNS down, etc.)
rate(certmanager_controller_sync_call_count{result="error"}[1h]) > 0

Pair the cert-expiry alert with a long window (7 days, not 24 hours) so you get paged during business hours, not at 03:00.

The renewal — end-to-end trace

1. cert-manager polls every cert it issued

Every ~hour by default, controlled by --renew-before-expiration-check-interval.

2. Spots one that crossed the renewal threshold

Subtracts renewBefore from notAfter. If we're past that point, schedule a renewal.

3. Creates a new Order & Challenge

Same flow as initial issuance: ACME order, HTTP-01 or DNS-01 challenge, solver does its thing.

4. New cert lands in the same Secret

The Secret.data is updated. Any pod mounting that Secret picks up the new cert on the next sync (usually within a minute).

5. Old cert / private key is discarded

cert-manager doesn't track cert history by default — once the new cert is in the Secret, the old one is gone. If you need history, use revisionHistoryLimit on the Certificate (cert-manager 1.15+).

Manual renewal & force-renew

Sometimes you want to renew now — the private key was leaked, you want to rotate, or a new CA root was deployed.

# trigger a renewal by annotating the Certificate
kubectl certificate renew api-example-com -n default

# or, if you don't have the cert-manager kubectl plugin, patch the Secret
kubectl delete secret api-example-com-tls -n default
# cert-manager re-issues within seconds because the Certificate still references the Secret

For emergency revocation (key compromised): the CA-specific revoke action via the issuer. ACME supports POST /acme/revoke-cert for LE-issued certs; private CAs have their own revocation APIs.
